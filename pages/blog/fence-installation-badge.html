The Basic Fence Installation Order

Most fence jobs follow this order: confirm the fence line, plan gates, call for utility locating, measure runs, mark corners and posts, set string lines, install posts, build sections, install gates, inspect the finished line, clean up, and walk the customer through the result.

  1. Confirm property line, utility, HOA, and city requirements.
  2. Measure fence runs and mark corners.
  3. Plan gate locations, swing direction, latch side, and access width.
  4. Run string lines and mark post locations.
  5. Dig holes and set posts for the material system.
  6. Install rails, panels, pickets, fabric, or boards.
  7. Build, hang, adjust, and test gates.
  8. Inspect line, level, hardware, tension, cleanup, and customer expectations.

In Utah County, soil conditions, slope, sprinklers, freeze-thaw movement, and high wind exposure can all affect fence quality. A straight layout is the base layer for every material.

Module 1

Terminology and Planning

Core Skills

  • Understand different types of fences
  • Measure fence lines accurately
  • Run string lines
  • Mark post locations
  • Lay out corners
  • Plan gate locations

Key Takeaways

  • Small layout mistakes become big installation mistakes.
  • Gate planning matters before holes are dug.
  • Measurements should be checked from the customer's use case, not just the material list.

Start with the purpose of the fence: privacy, pets, security, rental turnover, boundary cleanup, or a gate that needs to work every day. Then confirm the route, corners, slope, sprinkler heads, landscaping, access, and where gates need to open. A beautiful fence in the wrong line is still a failed job.

String lines are the crew's truth-check. Keep the string tight, visible, and protected from being kicked or moved. Mark posts consistently and double-check gate openings before digging. For homeowners, this is also the time to compare materials and restrictions before buying panels.

Module 2

Cedar Fence Installation

Core Skills

  • Rail installation
  • Picket spacing
  • Cutting lumber
  • Fastener placement
  • Working with warped lumber
  • Building gates
  • Keeping lines level

Key Takeaways

  • Wood moves over time.
  • Consistency matters visually.
  • A cedar gate needs structure, clearance, and hardware alignment, not just boards.

Cedar fence work is visual. Picket spacing, rail height, fastener lines, trimmed tops, and gate gaps all show from the yard and street. Sort lumber as you work, use the best pieces where they will be seen most, and do not let one badly warped board set the rhythm for a whole run.

Gate openings deserve extra attention. Set hinge posts solid, build the gate square, plan for sag, and test the swing before cleanup. For older wood fences, compare this module with our guide to repairing old fences before deciding whether a section should be rebuilt or patched.

Cedar Fence Production Checks

  • Rails are level or intentionally stepped with the grade.
  • Pickets have consistent spacing and height.
  • Fasteners are set cleanly without splitting boards.
  • Gate frame is square, braced, and clear of the ground.
  • Latch, hinge, and stop locations make sense for daily use.

Module 3

Vinyl Fence Installation

Core Skills

  • Post alignment
  • Panel installation
  • Rail systems
  • Expansion spacing
  • Gate installation

Key Takeaways

  • Vinyl shows mistakes quickly.
  • Precision matters more than speed.
  • Panel systems work best when the manufacturer's sequence is followed.

Vinyl fence installation is less forgiving than it looks. Posts must be aligned before panels go in, rail pockets need to line up, and panels need the expansion space the system calls for. Do not force panels to hide a layout problem; it usually makes the next section worse.

Vinyl gates require careful post support and hardware layout. Check swing, latch alignment, and gaps from normal standing height. White vinyl especially makes uneven reveals and dirty cleanup easy to notice.

Module 4

Composite Fence Installation

Core Skills

  • Panel assembly
  • Reinforcement requirements
  • Fastener systems
  • Gate installation
  • Material protection

Key Takeaways

  • Composite is heavier than many crews expect.
  • Follow manufacturer instructions exactly.
  • Protect finished faces from scratches, drag marks, and staging damage.

Composite fence systems are not generic wood fences with different boards. They often have specific post, channel, reinforcement, fastener, and gate requirements. Read the installation guide before committing to post spacing or gate openings, especially when mixing slopes, corners, and heavy panels.

Material handling is part of the finish. Stage panels on clean supports, keep hardware organized, and avoid dragging boards across concrete, gravel, or each other. If a system calls for a reinforcement, bracket, or fastener, do not improvise unless the manufacturer allows it.

Composite Fence Setup Checks

  • Manufacturer instructions are available on the jobsite.
  • Posts, inserts, rails, channels, and fasteners match the system.
  • Gate openings account for panel weight and hardware requirements.
  • Finished faces are protected while staging and cutting.
  • Cut edges and exposed components meet product guidance.

Module 5

Chain Link Installation

Core Skills

  • Terminal post installation
  • Top rail assembly
  • Fabric stretching
  • Tension bar installation
  • Tie wire installation
  • Gate installation

Key Takeaways

  • Tension determines quality.
  • Sagging means poor installation.
  • Terminal posts and gates carry more load than line posts.

Chain link quality is mostly about structure and tension. Terminal posts, corner posts, and gate posts need to be solid because fabric stretch pulls against them. Top rail should be continuous and clean, tension bars should be seated correctly, and tie wires should be consistent enough that the fence looks intentional.

When fabric is loose, the whole fence looks unfinished. Stretch carefully, check height, keep the bottom from waving, and inspect the gate after tension is applied. Gates should not drag, sag, or fight the latch.

Module 6

Tools and Safety

Core Skills

  • Auger safety
  • Saw safety
  • Impact driver use
  • PPE
  • Ladder safety
  • Lifting techniques
  • Underground utility awareness
  • Heat management

Key Takeaways

  • Safety creates production.
  • Injuries destroy efficiency.
  • Utility awareness is part of planning, not a last-minute step.

Fence work has real hazards: augers, saws, concrete, heavy panels, long rails, heat, uneven ground, and buried utilities. Keep PPE matched to the task, confirm utility locating before digging, and never rush a two-person lift just because a panel is almost in place.

Heat management matters in Utah summers. Start hydrated, rotate heavy tasks when possible, watch for dizziness or confusion, and plan shade or breaks before the crew is already overheated. Safe crews produce better work because they can think clearly.

Fence Job Safety Checklist

  • Utility locate requirements are handled before digging.
  • Eye, ear, hand, foot, and respiratory protection are available as needed.
  • Auger operators understand kickback, footing, and stop signals.
  • Saws are used on stable supports with clear hands and cords.
  • Panels, posts, and gates are lifted with enough people for the weight.
  • Water, shade, and heat breaks are planned on hot days.

Module 7

Quality Control and Professionalism

Core Skills

  • Checking level
  • Straight-line inspection
  • Gate testing
  • Hardware inspection
  • Cleanup standards
  • Customer communication
  • Protecting landscaping

Key Takeaways

  • Ask: does it look straight?
  • Ask: does the gate work perfectly?
  • Ask: would a homeowner notice problems?

Quality control starts before the customer points at anything. Step back and inspect the full run, not just the board or panel in front of you. Look from the street, from the patio, from the gate approach, and from wherever the homeowner will see the fence most often.

Professionalism is part of the finished product. Protect landscaping, manage debris, communicate schedule changes, explain any field adjustments, and leave the yard clean. A fence that looks good but leaves broken plants, trash, or a sticky gate still feels unfinished.

Final Fence Walkthrough Checklist

  • Fence line reads straight from normal viewing points.
  • Posts are plumb and panels or boards follow the intended grade plan.
  • Gates swing, latch, clear the ground, and stay closed as intended.
  • Hardware is tight, correctly placed, and visually clean.
  • Chain link fabric is tight with no obvious sag.
  • Vinyl or composite faces are clean and undamaged.
  • Scraps, concrete bags, cutoffs, loose fasteners, and old material are cleaned up.
  • Customer understands what was completed and what maintenance or curing time matters.

Q&A

Common Fence Questions

Can I use this guide to build a fence myself?

Yes, if the project is simple and you are comfortable with layout, digging, cutting, fastening, and cleanup. Before starting, confirm property lines, utilities, HOA rules, city requirements, and product instructions. When the fence includes long runs, difficult slope, heavy gates, or tight property-line questions, it is worth getting a professional look.

What causes the most fence callbacks?

Poor layout, weak posts, rushed gates, loose chain link tension, dirty cleanup, and small alignment mistakes that become obvious across a long run. Most of those problems are preventable when planning and final inspection are treated as real work.

Which fence type is best for Utah County homes?

It depends on the job. Cedar is flexible and familiar, vinyl is clean and low maintenance, composite can be a premium privacy option, and chain link is practical for pets, security, and budget-conscious boundaries. Start with the purpose, maintenance expectations, neighborhood requirements, and budget.
